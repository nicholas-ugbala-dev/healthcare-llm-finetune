* Fatigue or feeling weak
* Blurred vision
* Slow healing of cuts and wounds
* Tingling or numbness in hands and feet
* Unexplained weight loss
* Recurring infections
* Increased hunger
* Irritability or mood swings
* Headaches
* Unusual breath odor
It's essential to note that some people with type 2 diabetes may not exhibit any symptoms in the early stages. Therefore, it's crucial to get screened for diabetes at least once a year if you're over 45 years old, overweight, or have a family history of diabetes.

Q: What is the first-line treatment for hypertension in adults?
A: According to the latest guidelines from the American Heart Association (AHA) and the American College of Cardiology (ACC), the first-line treatment for hypertension in adults is lifestyle modification, followed by medication. Lifestyle modifications include:

*   Regular physical activity
*   Healthy diet
*   Weight loss (if needed)
*   Stress reduction
*   Adequate sleep

If lifestyle modifications alone are not sufficient to control blood pressure, medication is considered. The first-line medications for hypertension are:

*   Diuretics (e.g., hydrochlorothiazide)
*   Beta-blockers (e.g., metoprolol)
*   ACE inhibitors (e.g., lisinopril)
*   Calcium channel blockers (e.g., amlodipine)
*   ARBs (e.g., losartan)
*   SGLT-2 inhibitors (e.g., canaglifatran)

A combination of two or more medications is often used to achieve optimal blood pressure control. The goal is to achieve a systolic blood pressure of <130 mmHg and a diastolic blood pressure of <80 mmHg. Consult a healthcare professional to determine the best course of treatment.

Q: What causes iron deficiency anemia?
A: Iron deficiency anemia is a condition where the body does not have enough iron to produce hemoglobin, a protein in red blood cells that carries oxygen to different parts of the body. Iron deficiency anemia is the most common form of anemia and can occur in people of all ages. It can be caused by a variety of factors, including:

1. Poor diet: People who do not eat enough iron-rich foods, such as red meat, poultry, fish, beans, lentils, and fortified cereals, are at risk of developing iron deficiency anemia.
2. Menstruation: Women who have heavy menstrual periods or do not take iron supplements during their menstrual cycle may develop iron deficiency anemia.
3. Pregnancy: Pregnant women may develop iron deficiency anemia due to increased iron needs during pregnancy.
4. Gastrointestinal bleeding: People who have bleeding in their stomach or intestines may develop iron deficiency anemia due to loss of iron.
5. Infection: People who have infections such as malaria, tuberculosis, or HIV/AIDS may develop iron deficiency anemia due to increased iron needs.
6. Chronic diseases: People who have chronic diseases such as cancer, rheumatoid arthritis, or autoimmune disorders may develop iron deficiency anemia due to increased iron needs.
7. Medications: Certain medications, such as antacids, antibiotics, and anticoagulants, can interfere with iron absorption and lead to iron deficiency anemia.
8. Inadequate iron absorption: People who have conditions that affect iron absorption, such as celiac disease or Crohn's disease, may develop iron deficiency anemia.
9. Increased iron needs: People who have increased iron needs, such as athletes or people who are pregnant, may develop iron deficiency anemia if they do not consume enough iron-rich foods.
10. Genetic disorders: People who have genetic disorders such as thalassemia or sickle cell disease may develop iron deficiency anemia due to increased iron needs.

If you are concerned about iron deficiency anemia, consult with a healthcare professional who can evaluate your symptoms and provide treatment recommendations.

Q: How is malaria diagnosed and treated?
A: Malaria is a serious and sometimes life-threatening disease caused by the Plasmodium parasite. The diagnosis and treatment of malaria depend on the severity of the disease, the patient's age, and the patient's health status. Here are the steps involved in diagnosing and treating malaria:

1.  **Clinical Evaluation**: The doctor will evaluate the patient's symptoms and medical history to determine if they have malaria. The patient may experience fever, chills, headache, muscle pain, and fatigue.
2.  **Physical Examination**: The doctor will perform a physical examination to assess the patient's vital signs, such as blood pressure, heart rate, and respiratory rate.
3.  **Laboratory Tests**: The doctor may order laboratory tests to confirm the diagnosis of malaria. The tests may include a blood smear, a complete blood count (CBC), a urinalysis, and a rapid diagnostic test (RDT) or a polymerase chain reaction (PCR) test.
4.  **Treatment**: The treatment of malaria depends on the patient's age, weight, and health status. The treatment may include antimalarial medications, such as chloroquine, artemisinin, or mefloquine. The treatment may also include supportive care, such as hydration, rest, and pain management.
5.  **Follow-up**: The doctor will monitor the patient's progress and adjust the treatment as needed.

The symptoms of malaria can vary depending on the patient's age, weight, and health status. The symptoms may include:

*   Fever
*   Chills
*   Headache
*   Muscle pain
*   Fatigue
*   Nausea and vomiting
*   Abdominal pain
*   Diarrhea
*   Abdominal swelling
*   Lymphadenopathy (swelling of the lymph nodes)
*   Rash

The diagnosis and treatment of malaria are critical to prevent complications and deaths. If you suspect that you or a loved one has malaria, seek medical attention immediately.
